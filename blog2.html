Outis and the Inception of Tamriel's tamriel

Pain.

Throbbing, throbbing pain.

Each letter and word I make from this day on now lovingly carved by a drill piercing the heavens of my throbbing, inflamed wrist tendon. Wondeful. That's what I get for spending the last month obsessively trying to beat Daggerfall and Morrowind instead of producing actual content for the website I suppose. Alas!

It was not all for naught though. Through my obsessive labours, I discovered that The Elder Scroll games from Daggerfall onwards contains these interesting nested narratives in the form of in-game books. Some like the Lusty Argonian Maid contain worlds which interests the masses to the point of superseding the original via fan content, while others such as the Invocation of Azura rewards their reader by intergrating it's narrative into gameplay. (For example, in Daggerfall, this book was the only way you could figure out the right summoning dates! in-game!). Reading these books was a pleasure as I never bothered with doing so up until now.

After all, there's nothing more immersive than cracking open ABCs for Barbarians and pretending to read it out loud to soothe some angry naked Nord barbarian (Before you taunt him into attacking you so you can legally acquire his sweet sweet axe guilt-free) Or reading Warp in the West and realizing "Huh, so that's why Orcs are playable now...". My most favorite book however, is The Eastern Provinces Impartially Considered for the sole reason that it's all one giant scathing critique of the War on Terror, alluded through the voice of a disgruntled, suspiciously American-sounding Imperial scholar...

Indeed, one may argue that the inclusion of these books was to create this sense of immersion. To read a book through the simulcra inside the simulation nears the opus of The Matrix after all. However I find it more interesting that the nested narrative, or the story-within-the-story — creates the unique opportunity of providing one world to the existant game world. The short story to the digital adventure. The author to the gamer. All this creating a network of fiction, coalescing into a media gestalt. I'd dub this phenomena the "Elder Scrolls Cinematic Universe" would it not invariably forfeit my life to a hitherto unknown shadowy cabal of oligarchs and their legions of battle kaijin... So perhaps i'll just keep a lid on it for now. Regrettable.

So dear reader, was it worth contracting Carpal Tunnel Syndrome to learn all of this? I cannot say, as even now I am still immersed in the oh-so-delicious experience machine. Can you ask a butterfly if they still dream of being man? Maybe when university forces me out of the LCL will I finally be able to gain the courage to acknowledge my reflection within the confines of my jar. But until then, I think I will continue to swim in my embryonic cerebral fluid and delight in the joys of being a nobody.
